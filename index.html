Geophysicist main roles

Data Acquisition

Geophysicists are responsible for gathering various types of data related to the Earth's subsurface.

This may involve using specialized equipment such as seismographs, gravimeters, magnetometers, or ground-penetrating radar to collect data on seismic waves, gravity anomalies, magnetic fields, or electrical resistivity, among others.

Data Processing

Once the data is collected, geophysicists process it to extract meaningful information.

This often involves filtering, cleaning, and transforming raw data into a format that can be analyzed effectively. Data processing techniques may include filtering noise, removing artifacts, and applying mathematical algorithms to enhance signal clarity.

read more

Data Interpretation

Geophysicists analyze processed data to interpret subsurface geological structures, properties, and phenomena.

This requires knowledge of geology, physics, and mathematics to interpret seismic reflections, gravity anomalies, magnetic anomalies, resistivity variations, and other geophysical signals. Interpretations are used to generate models of subsurface features such as oil and gas reservoirs, mineral deposits, fault lines, and groundwater aquifers.

"The Present is The Key to the Past"

James Hutton

This phrase encapsulates Hutton's principle of uniformitarianism, which suggests that the processes we observe shaping the Earth's surface today have operated in much the same way over geological time scales. In other words, by studying present-day geological processes and features, scientists can gain insights into the Earth's past geological history. Hutton's ideas were revolutionary in his time and laid the foundation for the modern science of geology.

Analysis of Bathymetric Data Influence on Terrain Correction in Gravity Data Processing: Case Study in Banda Neira and Kur Island Regions


The results indicated that terrain correction calculations involving bathymetric data significantly contributed to the maximum total terrain correction values. The difference reached approximately 81,5 mGal in Banda Neira region and 69,8 mGal in Kur Island region. However, concerning correction patterns, the contribution of bathymetric data was inversely related to the contribution of topographic data when correlated with the topographic conditions. In regions further away from the Weber Trench, like Banda Neira, the inclusion of bathymetric data showed a terrain correction pattern similar to using only topographic data.

However, in the case of Kur Island, which is closer to the Weber Trench, the inclusion of bathymetric data has a more significant impact and is capable of reversing the terrain correction pattern when using only topographic data. This makes bathymetric data worthy of consideration for high-resolution gravitational data processing, particularly in areas with extensive and deep trenches or seas nearby.



Estimating Earthquake Hypocenter Using Inversion Approach


Scenario: Imagine a scenario where a seismic event occurs and is monitored by a network of four seismic stations. These stations are equipped with seismometers to detect ground motions caused by earthquakes. The stations are positioned at known coordinates (xs, ys, zs) in a three-dimensional space.

Objective: The objective of this case study is to determine the hypocenter of the earthquake. The hypocenter represents the point of origin of seismic waves within the Earth's crust. By estimating the hypocenter, we aim to understand the earthquake's source and assess potential seismic hazards.

The inversion process aims to refine the initial estimate of the earthquake's hypocenter by iteratively minimizing the misfit between observed and calculated arrival times of seismic waves. Through this iterative optimization process, a more accurate estimate of the hypocenter is obtained, allowing for a better understanding of the earthquake's source characteristics


Variability in Stratigraphy and Geological Structures of Lembang Fault: A Comprehensive Overview


The stratigraphy found in the Lembang Fault exhibits quite a notable variation. For instance, in the Sungai Cimeta area, there are three layers:

  • Layer 1 (Yellow): Comprised of sediment breccia found in river channels with sand, tuff, and andesite fragments.
  • Layer 2 (Red): Consists of andesite rock with barely visible crystals, dark grayish ash-colored, and extrusive in nature.
  • Layer 3 (Blue): Constitutes andesite rock with a brownish-gray ash color.


Additionally, in Goa Jepang Tahura, there is an exposure of pyroclastic breccia measuring 50 meters in length and 8 meters in height. This breccia exhibits slightly weathered conditions, open packing, poor sorting, and fragments in lapilli (2-64 mm) and block (> 64 mm) forms. These fragments are composed of scoria.


The geological structure of the Lembang Fault is also clearly observed, especially in Sungai Cimeta and Gunung Batu. In Sungai Cimeta, there is a fault line structure that causes the stratigraphic exposure to be non-parallel, along with sheeting joints. In Gunung Batu, there are sheeting joints/foliated fractures with dimensions of 250 meters in length and 20 meters in height. The underlying rocks found there are brownish-gray in color with a porphyritic texture, representing andesite rock.


The geological history of the Lembang Fault itself is formed by the eruption of Mount Sunda Purba, which created a large basin in the middle of the present-day city of Bandung. The magma present in this location resulted in the formation of faults cutting through the basin. The steep slopes in the Lembang area are also the result of lava flows from the eruption of Mount Tangkuban Perahu. The scattered rocks consist of pyroclastic material with a flow pattern, as seen in the excursion to Goa Jepang. In other locations such as Sungai Cimeta and Gunung Batu, exposures of andesite rock are formed by magma cooling at the surface, resulting in the formation of sheeting joints.
